CRPS
The continuous rank probability score is defined as the quadratic distance between the forecasted CDF F^(z)\hat{F}(z)F^(z)﻿ and the empirical one. That is, CRPS computes how close the predicted quantiles are w.r.t. the data distribution:
Definition
CRPS⁡(f,y)=∫−∞∞(F^(z)−I{y≤z})2dz\begin{aligned}\operatorname{CRPS}(f, y)&=\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} (\hat{F}(z)-\mathbb{I}\{y \leq z\})^2 \mathrm{d} z \end{aligned}CRPS(f,y)​=∫−∞∞​(F^(z)−I{y≤z})2dz​
Alternative formulation
∫01ρτ(F^−1(τ),y)dτ\int_0^1 \rho_\tau\left(\hat{F}^{-1}(\tau), y\right) \mathrm{d} \tau∫01​ρτ​(F^−1(τ),y)dτ
The CRPS is a proper scoring rule, hence its minimum is obtained when F^(z)\hat{F}(z) F^(z)﻿ and the data distribution are equal
The first expression states that the CRPS can be computed by Montecarlo sampling from F^(z)\hat{F}(z)F^(z)﻿​
The second expression states that CRPS can be computed as the mean value of the quantile score over equispaced predicted quantiles F^−1\hat{F}^{-1}F^−1﻿​
Since CRSP depends on the scale of the signal, in order to compare it across different time series it must be normalized with a benchmark forecaster:

rτ(q^τ,y)=1N∑i=1NI{y<q^τ}r_{\tau}(\hat{q}_\tau, y) = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N} \mathbb{I}_{\{y<\hat{q}_\tau\}}rτ​(q^​τ​,y)=N1​i=1∑N​I{y<q^​τ​}​

Conformal predictions
Conformal prediction is based on the idea of constructing a set of hypothesis tests to determine whether a new observation is consistent with the previously observed data. The test results are then used to construct a prediction interval, which is guaranteed to contain the true value with a certain probability.
Conformal prediction doesn’t require IID assumption, but requires exchangeability: any permutation of the dataset observations is equiprobable. However, the time-steps within a time-series are inherently non-exchangeable due to temporal dependencies.
Given a dataset D={(x(i),y(i))}i=1l\mathcal{D}=\left\{\left(\mathbf{x}^{(i)}, y^{(i)}\right)\right\}_{i=1}^lD={(x(i),y(i))}i=1l​﻿, CP generates prediction intervals such that:
P[yt+1∈Γα(xt+1)∣D]≥1−α\mathbb{P}\left[y_{t+1} \in \Gamma^\alpha\left(\mathbf{x}_{t+1}\right) \mid \mathcal{D}\right] \geq 1-\alphaP[yt+1​∈Γα(xt+1​)∣D]≥1−α
Left: time series can’t be considered exchangeable. Right: exchangeability can be obtained considering several instances of multiple step-ahead forecasting (=embeddings).
CP theory is pretty involved, but the steps of the basic method can be summarized as:
Split the dataset in training and calibration D=Dtrain ∪Dcal \mathcal{D}=\mathcal{D}_{\text {train }} \cup \mathcal{D}_{\text {cal }}D=Dtrain ​∪Dcal ​﻿​
Train a forecasting model MMM﻿ on Dtrain \mathcal{D}_{\text {train }} Dtrain ​﻿​
Use the calibration set Dcal\mathcal{D}_{\text {cal}} Dcal​﻿ to obtain a non-conformity score: a measure of how much new predictions differ from observed ones. Usually the non-conformity score is the absolute value of the prediction error.
Compute prediction intervals as Γα(xt+1)=[y^t+1−ε^,y^t+1+ε^]\Gamma^\alpha\left(\mathbf{x}_{t+1}\right)=\left[\hat{y}_{t+1}-\hat{\varepsilon}, \hat{y}_{t+1}+\hat{\varepsilon}\right]Γα(xt+1​)=[y^​t+1​−ε^,y^​t+1​+ε^]﻿ where y^t+1=M(xt+1)\hat{y}_{t+1} = M(\mathbf{x}_{t+1})y^​t+1​=M(xt+1​)﻿ and ε^\hat{\varepsilon} ε^﻿ is the 1−α1-\alpha1−α﻿ quantile of the prediction error on Dcal\mathcal{D}_{\text {cal}} Dcal​﻿, ordered with the conformity score.
CP intervals for multi step-ahead predictions: each step ahead has its own error distribution
When the non-conformity score is the prediction error, this method corresponds to the naive bootstrap method.
The only difference is that for multi step-ahead forecasts CI applies a Bonferroni correction: instead of taking the τ\tauτ﻿ quantile, we take the τ±(1−τ)/ns\tau \pm (1-\tau)/n_{s}τ±(1−τ)/ns​﻿ quantile, where the sign depends on whether we are computing a quantile lower (-) than 0.5 or higher (+).
